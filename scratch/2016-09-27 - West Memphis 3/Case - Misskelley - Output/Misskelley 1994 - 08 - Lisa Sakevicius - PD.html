[10] Q. Will you please state you name and occupation?

[12] Q. What education, experience and training have you had to qualify you as a criminalist at the Crime Lab?

[13] A. I have a degree in chemistry from the University of Central Arkansas. I worked as a chemist for a year at the Arkansas Plant Board where I learned to use several instruments. I've had polarized light microscopy courses from the McKrohn Institute and manmade fiber identifications from McKrohn Institute. [139] BY MR. FOGLEMAN:

[140] Q. Just so we're real clear on all this, on the Cub Scout cap, which is State's Exhibit 8, you found a fiber microscopically similar to the fibers in State's Exhibit 85 which is a shirt from Damien Echols' residence?

[141] A. Correct.

[142] Q. And then you found a fiber on the white polka-dot shirt, State's Exhibit 44, which was consistent with the fiber from the red housecoat from Jason Baldwin's house?

[143] A. That's correct.

[144] Q. And then you found a green polyester fiber on the pair of blue pants labeled State's Exhibit 45 that was microscopically similar to the same shirt from Damien Echols?

[145] A. Polyester and cotton.

[146] Q. Two fibers. One cotton, one polyester?

[147] A. Correct.

[148] Q. I noted the fibers were what color?

[149] A. Green.

[150] Q. And the shirt is blue. Explain that to me.

[151] A. The color that I see under the microscope isn't necessarily the overall color that it might appear to you. If I have a glass of water, it appears clear but if you look at the ocean, it appears blue. That's the difference in my terminology from what I'm seeing.

[152] Q. So what exactly are you looking at when you are looking at -- when you're making a fiber comparison?

[153] A. I look at both the fibers side by side in a comparison microscope. I examine the color. I look at a property called birefringence. I look at delustrants. I take them to another instrument called a microspectrophotometer. I examine to make sure the dyes are similar. I take them if they are synthetic to another instrument called a fourier transform infrared microscope and examine them there to make sure the polymers are the same.

[154] Q. So it is not just a matter of looking at them under the microscope and saying they are similar?

[155] A. Correct.

[156] Q. In regard to the fiber on the housecoat or fibers in the housecoat that match the housecoat from Jason Baldwin's house and the fiber found on Exhibit 44, which was the black and white shirt, is that a common type fiber that you see in the lab?

[157] A. I don't see it as often as I do a lot of the other types.

[158] CROSS EXAMINATION

[159] BY MR. STIDHAM:

[160] Q. Can you tell the jury what microscopically similar means?

[161] A. That I cannot distinguish the two. They look the same to me.

[162] Q. Does that mean it is a definite match or just similar?

[163] A. That means there are no distinguishable differences between the two fibers.

[164] Q. Are you able to exclude all other possibilities between the two? In other words does it definitely come from that source because it's similar?

[165] A. It does not necessarily mean it comes from that source. There could be a number of items containing that same fiber type so we can never say it came from a particular source.

[166] Q. So we are not talking about exactness then, are we.

[167] A. It can be exactness in that the fibers are alike, but you cannot say that it came from a particular source.

[168] Q. You cannot exclude all other sources?

[169] A. Correct.

[170] Q. Were any of these fibers you just mentioned -- were they similar to anything else that you looked into or compared?
